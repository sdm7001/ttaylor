**Version**: 1.0
**Jurisdiction**: Harris County, Houston, Texas
**Last Updated**: 2026-04-20

## A

### ACP (Address Confidentiality Program)
A Texas program administered by the Attorney General's office that provides substitute addresses to victims of family violence, sexual assault, stalking, or trafficking. Participants receive a substitute mailing address so their actual physical location is not disclosed in court filings or public records. When a party is enrolled in ACP, the platform must suppress their true address from all documents, filings, and portal views, substituting the ACP-designated address instead.

### Ad Litem
An attorney or guardian appointed by the court to represent the interests of a specific party — most commonly a child — in a legal proceeding. In Texas family law, there are two distinct types: an **Attorney Ad Litem** (represents the child's expressed wishes) and a **Guardian Ad Litem** (represents the child's best interests, which may differ from the child's stated preferences). See also: Guardian ad Litem, Amicus Attorney.

### Affidavit
A written statement of facts made under oath and signed before a notary public or other authorized officer. In family law, affidavits are commonly used to support motions (e.g., Affidavit of Inability to Pay, Affidavit of Relinquishment of Parental Rights), verify financial information (e.g., Financial Information Statement), or provide evidence without live testimony at temporary orders hearings.

### Agreed Order
A court order that both parties have negotiated and consented to, typically presented to the judge for approval and signature. Agreed orders bypass the need for a contested hearing but still require judicial review. The court retains discretion to reject an agreed order if it does not serve the best interest of the child or violates public policy. Distinguished from a Mediated Settlement Agreement (MSA), which has stronger enforceability protections under Texas Family Code § 153.0071.

### Amicus Attorney
An attorney appointed by the court under Texas Family Code § 107.003 to assist the court in protecting the best interests of a child. Unlike an Attorney Ad Litem, the Amicus Attorney does not represent the child as a client but instead provides the court with information and recommendations. The Amicus Attorney may interview the child, parents, and other relevant persons, review records, and make recommendations to the court. Their communications with the child are not privileged.

### Arbitration
A private dispute resolution process where the parties agree to submit their disagreement to an arbitrator who renders a binding or non-binding decision. In Texas family law, arbitration is authorized under Texas Family Code § 153.0071 and the Texas General Arbitration Act. Certain issues — particularly conservatorship and possession of children — have limitations on arbitrability. Arbitration awards are subject to judicial review for errors of law.

### Associate Judge
A judicial officer appointed by the presiding judge of a district court or statutory county court to hear and decide certain categories of cases. In Harris County, associate judges handle a significant portion of family law dockets, including temporary orders hearings, enforcement actions, and child support matters. Parties have the right to de novo review by the referring court of an associate judge's ruling within a specified time period (typically 3 days for some matters, 30 days for others under Texas Family Code Chapter 201).

### Attachment
In the context of e-filing, an attachment is a supplementary document included with the lead document in a filing packet. Examples include exhibits to a motion, proposed orders accompanying a motion, or supporting affidavits. Harris County guidance requires that affidavits accompanying a principal document (such as a verification on a petition) be filed as a single combined document rather than as separate attachments. See also: Filing Packet, Lead Document.

## C

### Case Style
The formal heading of a legal case that appears on all filed documents, identifying the parties and the court. In Texas family law, the case style typically follows the format: "In the Matter of the Marriage of [Petitioner] and [Respondent]" for divorces, or "In the Interest of [Child's Name], a Child" for SAPCR cases. The case style must exactly match the court's records in all filings.

### Cause
A legal proceeding filed in a court. In Texas, each cause is assigned a unique cause number and tracked through the court's docket. A single matter in the platform may correspond to one or more causes (e.g., a divorce may also have a related protective order cause).

### Cause Number
A unique identifier assigned by the district clerk to each case filed in a Harris County court. The cause number format varies by court but generally follows a pattern that encodes the court number and a sequential filing number (e.g., "2026-12345" for district courts). The cause number is required on every document filed with the court and is the primary key for locating a case in the Odyssey case management system.

### Child Support
Court-ordered periodic payments made by the obligor (non-custodial parent) to the obligee (custodial parent or managing conservator) for the financial support of a child. In Texas, child support is calculated under Texas Family Code Chapter 154 using a percentage-of-income model based on the obligor's net monthly resources: 20% for one child, 25% for two, 30% for three, 35% for four, 40% for five or more. The statutory cap on net resources subject to the percentage guidelines is adjusted annually. Child support obligations generally continue until the child turns 18 or graduates high school (whichever is later), marries, is emancipated, or dies.

### Citation
The formal notice issued by the district clerk informing a respondent that a suit has been filed against them. In Texas, citation must be served in strict compliance with the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure (Rules 99-107). Citation must include a copy of the petition and inform the respondent of the deadline to file an answer (typically the first Monday after 20 days from service). Failure to properly serve citation can render any subsequent default judgment voidable.

### Conflict Check
The process of searching the firm's records to determine whether representing a prospective client would create a conflict of interest with an existing or former client. In family law, conflict checks are especially critical because the same firm cannot represent both spouses in a divorce, and prior representation of a family member in a related matter may create disqualifying conflicts. The platform must search across all matter types, including matters that have been closed or declined, and flag potential conflicts for attorney review. See also: Conflict of Interest.

### Conflict of Interest
A situation where an attorney's duties to one client conflict with the attorney's duties to another client, a former client, or the attorney's own interests. Governed by the Texas Disciplinary Rules of Professional Conduct (Rules 1.06, 1.09). In family law, conflicts commonly arise from prior representation of a spouse, family member, or opposing party. Conflicts can be waivable (with informed written consent) or non-waivable (where representation would be prohibited regardless of consent). The platform must maintain conflict records indefinitely, including for declined matters.

### Conservatorship
The legal framework in Texas that defines the rights, duties, and authority of parents (or other parties) regarding a child. Texas does not use the terms "custody" or "visitation" — instead, it uses "conservatorship" and "possession and access." There are three primary types:

- **JMC (Joint Managing Conservatorship)**: Both parents are appointed as joint managing conservators, sharing rights and duties. This is the statutory presumption in Texas (Texas Family Code § 153.131). The court designates one parent as the conservator with the exclusive right to determine the child's primary residence, typically with a geographic restriction.

- **SMC (Sole Managing Conservatorship)**: One parent is appointed sole managing conservator with the majority of rights and duties, while the other is named possessory conservator. Awarded when the court finds JMC would not be in the child's best interest, often involving family violence, substance abuse, or abandonment.

- **PMC (Possessory Conservatorship)**: The non-primary parent in a conservatorship arrangement. A possessory conservator has the right to possession and access to the child according to a court-ordered schedule but has limited decision-making authority.

### County Court at Law
A statutory court in Harris County with concurrent jurisdiction over certain family law matters. Harris County has multiple County Civil Courts at Law, some of which are designated to hear family law cases. These courts share jurisdiction with district courts for matters involving divorces, SAPCR suits, and protective orders. The distinction between filing in a district court versus a county court at law affects procedural rules, filing fees, and appeal routes.

### CSRP (Child Support Review Process)
An administrative process conducted by the Texas Attorney General's Office, Child Support Division, to review and adjust child support orders without requiring a full court hearing. Either parent can request a review every three years (or sooner if there has been a material and substantial change in circumstances). The CSRP evaluates current income and circumstances and issues a proposed modified order. If both parties agree, the modification is submitted to the court for approval. If either party disagrees, the matter proceeds to a contested hearing.

## D

### Decree
A formal written order of the court that resolves the substantive issues in a case. In family law, the most common decree is the Final Decree of Divorce, which dissolves the marriage and addresses all issues including property division, conservatorship, possession, child support, and spousal maintenance. A decree is a final, appealable order (as opposed to temporary orders, which are interlocutory).

### Discovery
The pre-trial phase of litigation where parties exchange information and evidence relevant to the case. In Texas family law, discovery is governed by the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure (Rules 190-215) and includes:

- **RFPs (Requests for Production)**: Requests for the opposing party to produce documents, electronically stored information, or tangible things for inspection and copying. Commonly used to obtain financial records, communications, and property documentation.

- **Interrogatories**: Written questions served on the opposing party that must be answered under oath within 30 days. Texas limits interrogatories to 25 per party (including discrete subparts) under Level 2 discovery.

- **RFAs (Requests for Admissions)**: Written statements served on the opposing party that must be admitted or denied within 30 days. Failure to respond results in the matters being deemed admitted. Commonly used to establish undisputed facts and narrow trial issues.

- **Subpoena**: A court-ordered command requiring a person to appear for testimony (subpoena ad testificandum) or produce documents (subpoena duces tecum). In Texas, subpoenas in family law cases are issued under Rule 176 of the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure.

### District Court
A court of general jurisdiction in Texas established under the Texas Constitution. Harris County has multiple district courts designated to hear family law matters (the 245th, 246th, 247th, 257th, 308th, 309th, 310th, 311th, 312th, and 313th Judicial District Courts, among others). District courts have original jurisdiction over divorces, SAPCR suits, adoptions, and other family law matters. Each district court maintains its own docket and may have specific local rules and procedures.

### Docket
The schedule or calendar of cases pending before a court. In Harris County, each district court and county court at law maintains its own docket. The docket includes hearing dates, trial settings, status conferences, and other scheduled proceedings. The platform must track docket entries for each matter and synchronize with the court's calendar for deadline management.

### DRO (Harris County Domestic Relations Office)
The Harris County Domestic Relations Office provides court-ordered services including social studies, custody evaluations, drug testing, supervised visitation, and mediation. The DRO is a department of the Harris County court system and operates under the supervision of the family district courts. DRO reports and recommendations carry significant weight with judges. Common DRO-related workflow steps include requesting a social study, scheduling drug testing, and tracking DRO report deadlines.

## E

### e-Filing
The mandatory electronic submission of documents to Texas courts through an approved Electronic Filing Service Provider (EFSP). Since January 1, 2014, e-filing has been mandatory in all Texas civil courts. All documents — petitions, motions, orders, and supporting documents — must be filed electronically unless a specific exemption applies (e.g., documents filed by self-represented litigants who qualify for an exemption). See also: eFileTexas, Odyssey.

### eFileTexas
The statewide e-filing portal (eFileTexas.gov) operated by Tyler Technologies that serves as the primary interface for submitting electronic filings to Texas courts. eFileTexas connects to the court's case management system (Odyssey) and processes filing packets, calculates filing fees, and routes documents to the appropriate court clerk for review. The platform must generate filing packets that comply with eFileTexas formatting, size, and metadata requirements.

### Engagement Letter
A written agreement between an attorney and client that establishes the terms of the attorney-client relationship, including scope of representation, fee structure, billing practices, retainer requirements, and termination provisions. In Texas, engagement letters are required by good practice (though not strictly mandated by the disciplinary rules for all engagements) and serve as the foundation of the client relationship. The platform generates engagement letters as part of the intake workflow, tracks their execution status, and links them to the matter record.

### Envelope
In the context of e-filing through eFileTexas, an envelope is the top-level container for a filing submission. An envelope contains one or more filing packets, each of which includes a lead document and optional attachments. The envelope also carries metadata including the case number, court, filing code, party information, and payment details. A single envelope may contain multiple filings for the same case (e.g., a motion and a proposed order). The platform models envelopes as first-class objects to ensure proper grouping and submission.

## F

### Filing Packet
A structured collection of documents submitted together as a single filing through eFileTexas. Each filing packet consists of a lead document (the primary document being filed, such as a motion or petition), zero or more attachments (supporting documents, exhibits, proposed orders), and metadata (filing code, document type, confidentiality designation). Harris County has specific rules about how documents must be grouped within filing packets — for example, affidavits that verify a petition must be combined with the petition as a single document rather than filed as separate attachments. See also: Lead Document, Attachment, Envelope.

### Final Decree of Divorce
The court order that formally dissolves a marriage and resolves all attendant issues including property division, debt allocation, conservatorship, possession and access, child support, medical support, spousal maintenance, and name changes. The Final Decree of Divorce is a comprehensive document that must address every issue raised in the pleadings. In Texas, the decree cannot be signed until at least 60 days after the original petition was filed (the "cooling off" period under Texas Family Code § 6.702) unless a family violence waiver applies. The decree must be approved as to form and substance by all parties or rendered after a contested trial.

## G

### Guardian ad Litem
A person appointed by the court under Texas Family Code § 107.001 to represent the best interests of a child in a suit. Unlike an Attorney Ad Litem (who advocates the child's expressed wishes), the Guardian ad Litem advocates for what they determine to be in the child's best interest, even if that differs from the child's stated preferences. A Guardian ad Litem need not be an attorney — the court may appoint a qualified volunteer or professional. In Harris County, CASA (Court Appointed Special Advocates) volunteers frequently serve in this role. The Guardian ad Litem conducts an independent investigation and files a written report with the court.

## I

### Income Withholding Order (IWO)
A court order or administrative order directing an employer to withhold a specified amount from an obligor's earnings for child support or spousal maintenance payments. In Texas, income withholding is the primary method of child support collection and is mandatory in most cases under Texas Family Code § 158.001. The IWO is sent directly to the obligor's employer, who must begin withholding no later than the first pay period after receiving the order. The withheld amounts are remitted to the Texas State Disbursement Unit (SDU). See also: Writ of Withholding.

### Intake
The process of gathering information from a prospective client, evaluating whether the firm can represent them, and onboarding them as a client. In the platform, intake is a structured workflow that includes: (1) initial contact capture (lead creation), (2) conflict check, (3) initial consultation scheduling, (4) client interview and information gathering, (5) engagement decision, (6) engagement letter execution, (7) retainer collection, and (8) matter creation. Intake is the gateway through which all new matters enter the system.

### Intervenor
A third party who petitions the court for permission to participate in an existing case. In Texas family law, intervenors are most common in SAPCR cases, where grandparents, other relatives, or non-parent caregivers seek conservatorship or possession rights. Intervention requires filing a Petition in Intervention and demonstrating standing under the applicable provisions of the Texas Family Code (e.g., § 102.004 for grandparents). The court may grant or deny intervention based on standing and the best interest of the child.

### IOLTA (Interest on Lawyers' Trust Accounts)
A type of trust account required by the Texas State Bar where client funds (retainers, settlement proceeds, etc.) are held separately from the firm's operating funds. Interest earned on IOLTA accounts is remitted to the Texas Access to Justice Foundation to fund legal aid. The platform must track all client funds deposited into and disbursed from IOLTA accounts, maintain a detailed ledger for each client, and ensure that client funds are never commingled with firm funds. See also: Trust Account, Retainer.

## L

### Lead
A prospective client who has made initial contact with the firm but has not yet been converted to an active client. In the platform, a lead represents the earliest stage of the client lifecycle — before conflict checks, consultations, or engagement decisions. Leads are tracked with contact information, referral source, matter type interest, and status. A lead becomes a client when an engagement letter is executed and a matter is created.

### Lead Document
In the context of e-filing, the lead document is the primary document in a filing packet — the document that the filing is "about." For example, in a filing that includes a Motion to Modify and a proposed order, the Motion to Modify is the lead document and the proposed order is an attachment. The lead document determines the filing code, document type, and filing fee for the packet. Every filing packet must have exactly one lead document. See also: Filing Packet, Attachment.

### Legal Assistant
A member of the law office support staff who performs administrative and clerical tasks under the supervision of an attorney. In Texas, the distinction between a paralegal and a legal assistant is functional: legal assistants typically handle scheduling, filing, correspondence, and client communication, while paralegals perform more substantive legal work. The platform assigns different permission levels to Legal Assistant and Paralegal roles to reflect this distinction.

### Local Rules
Supplementary rules adopted by individual courts or court districts that govern practice and procedure in addition to the statewide Texas Rules of Civil Procedure. Harris County family courts have specific local rules covering topics such as scheduling, discovery deadlines, temporary orders hearing procedures, agreed order submission requirements, and e-filing conventions. The platform must account for local rules in its deadline calculations and workflow configurations.

## M

### Matter
The central organizing entity in the platform, representing a legal engagement between the firm and a client. A matter encompasses all work product, documents, communications, deadlines, and billing associated with a specific legal proceeding or transaction. Each matter has a type (e.g., Divorce, SAPCR, Adoption), a status (e.g., Pre-Filing, Active, Pending Final Order, Closed), and is linked to one or more contacts (clients, opposing parties, attorneys). A single client may have multiple matters, and a single cause number may be associated with multiple matters (e.g., an original SAPCR matter and a subsequent modification matter under the same cause number).

### Mediation
A structured negotiation process facilitated by a neutral third-party mediator. In Harris County family law, mediation is frequently ordered by the court before trial and is required in many cases under local rules. Mediation is confidential under Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code § 154.073. If mediation results in a full agreement, the parties sign a Mediated Settlement Agreement (MSA), which is binding and enforceable under Texas Family Code § 153.0071 (for matters involving children) or § 6.602 (for property division). If mediation is unsuccessful, the case proceeds to trial.

### Medical Support
A component of a child support order requiring one or both parents to provide health insurance coverage for the child and/or contribute to the cost of the child's healthcare. Under Texas Family Code Chapter 154, Subchapter D, the court must order medical support as part of every child support order. Medical support can include: (1) health insurance coverage through an employer or government program, (2) cash medical support (a monthly amount to reimburse insurance premiums), and (3) payment of unreimbursed medical expenses.

### Motion
A formal written request to the court for a specific ruling or order. In Texas family law, common motions include: Motion for Temporary Orders, Motion to Modify, Motion to Enforce, Motion to Compel Discovery, Motion for Continuance, Motion for Sanctions, and Motion to Withdraw. Motions must comply with the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure regarding form, service, and notice requirements. Most motions require a hearing, though some (such as agreed motions) may be ruled on without a hearing.

### Motion to Enforce
A motion filed when a party has failed to comply with the terms of an existing court order. In Texas family law, enforcement mechanisms include contempt (Texas Family Code Chapter 157), which can result in fines or jail time for willful violations; clarification (Chapter 157, Subchapter D); and specific enforcement provisions for property division orders (Texas Family Code § 9.006-9.014). The motion must specifically identify the order provision violated, the manner of violation, and the relief sought.

### Motion to Modify
A motion seeking to change the terms of an existing court order. In Texas family law, modification of conservatorship, possession, or child support requires showing a material and substantial change in circumstances since the prior order (Texas Family Code § 156.101). For child support, modification may also be sought if it has been three or more years since the last order and the current amount differs by 20% or $100 from the guidelines amount. Modification of property division from a final decree is generally not permitted except in narrow fraud or procedural circumstances.

### MSA (Mediated Settlement Agreement)
A written agreement reached by the parties during mediation that is signed by each party and their attorney (if any). Under Texas Family Code § 153.0071, an MSA that meets statutory requirements is binding on the parties and is not subject to revocation. The court must render an order in conformity with the MSA unless the court finds (on motion by a party) that the MSA was procured by fraud, duress, coercion, or other dishonest means, or that the agreement is not in the best interest of a child (the court's discretion on child-related terms is preserved). An MSA is a critical workflow artifact — once signed, it triggers the drafting of final orders that conform to its terms.

## O

### Odyssey
The court case management system used by Harris County courts, developed by Tyler Technologies. Odyssey maintains the official record of all cases, filings, orders, and docket entries. e-Filed documents are routed through eFileTexas to Odyssey for clerk review and acceptance. The platform may need to reference Odyssey case data (case status, hearing dates, filed documents) for workflow management and deadline tracking.

### Order
A written directive issued by a court that commands or authorizes a specific action. Orders may be temporary (effective during the pendency of the case) or final (resolving the case). In Texas family law, common orders include Temporary Orders, Protective Orders, Income Withholding Orders, and Final Decrees. All orders must be signed by the judge (or associate judge) and filed with the clerk. The platform tracks orders as distinct document types with their own lifecycle and enforceability metadata.

## P

### Paralegal
A person qualified by education, training, or work experience who performs substantive legal work under the supervision of an attorney. In Texas, paralegals are governed by the Paralegal Division of the State Bar of Texas and may not provide legal advice, represent clients in court, or set legal fees. However, paralegals perform critical functions including document drafting, legal research, client communication, filing management, and case preparation. The platform is designed primarily for paralegal users, with workflows that route attorney-required actions (such as document approval and filing authorization) through explicit approval gates.

### Parenting Plan
A detailed plan addressing conservatorship, possession and access, decision-making rights, and communication protocols for parents of a child. While Texas does not require a formal "parenting plan" document, many Harris County courts encourage or require detailed parenting provisions in final orders. A parenting plan typically addresses: regular possession schedule, holiday and vacation schedules, transportation arrangements, communication with the child during the other parent's possession, right of first refusal, extracurricular activities, and protocols for resolving future disagreements.

### Petitioner
The party who initiates a legal proceeding by filing the original petition with the court. In a divorce, the petitioner is the spouse who files the Original Petition for Divorce. In a SAPCR, the petitioner may be a parent, the Attorney General, or another party with standing. The petitioner bears the initial burden of establishing the court's jurisdiction and stating a claim for relief.

### Possession Schedule
The court-ordered schedule that governs when each parent has physical possession of a child. Texas Family Code Chapter 153, Subchapter F establishes the Standard Possession Order (SPO), which is the default schedule unless the court finds it is not in the child's best interest. The SPO provides the possessory conservator with: first, third, and fifth weekends of each month; Thursday evenings; alternating holidays; and extended summer possession. Parents may agree to a modified or expanded possession schedule. The platform must track possession schedules and generate calendar entries.

### Privilege
The legal right to withhold information or communications from disclosure. In family law, the most relevant privileges are: (1) Attorney-Client Privilege (communications between attorney and client made for the purpose of legal advice are confidential), (2) Work Product Doctrine (materials prepared in anticipation of litigation are protected), and (3) Spousal Privilege (under limited circumstances in Texas). The platform must flag privileged materials and prevent their inadvertent disclosure in discovery or e-filing.

### Proof of Service
Documentation establishing that a party was properly served with legal papers. In Texas, proof of service may take the form of a Return of Service (filed by the process server), an Affidavit of Service, or a Certificate of Service. Proof of service is a critical workflow gate — the case cannot proceed to default judgment or certain hearings without valid proof that the opposing party was served.

### Protective Order
A court order issued under Texas Family Code Chapter 85 to protect a victim of family violence, sexual assault, stalking, or trafficking. Protective orders can prohibit the respondent from committing further violence, threatening the applicant, communicating with the applicant, going near the applicant's residence or workplace, and possessing firearms. Protective orders can be issued on a temporary (ex parte) basis or after a hearing. Violation of a protective order is a criminal offense. See also: TRO.

## Q

### QDRO (Qualified Domestic Relations Order)
A court order that divides a retirement plan or pension between divorcing spouses. A QDRO is required to divide most employer-sponsored retirement plans (401(k), pension, etc.) without triggering early withdrawal penalties or tax consequences. The QDRO must meet both the requirements of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) and the specific plan's requirements. The drafting of QDROs is a specialized task — the platform tracks QDRO status as part of the post-decree implementation workflow and ensures the QDRO is pre-approved by the plan administrator before submission to the court.

## R

### Respondent
The party against whom a legal proceeding is brought. In a divorce, the respondent is the non-filing spouse. In a SAPCR, the respondent is typically the other parent. The respondent must be properly served with citation and a copy of the petition and has the right to file an answer and counter-petition within the time prescribed by the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure.

### Retainer
An advance payment made by a client to the firm to secure legal representation and fund anticipated legal fees and expenses. In Texas, retainers are deposited into the firm's IOLTA trust account and are drawn down as fees are earned and expenses incurred. The platform tracks retainer balances, generates replenishment notices when balances fall below a threshold, and maintains a detailed trust ledger for each client. Earned fees are transferred from the trust account to the firm's operating account. See also: IOLTA, Trust Account.

### Return of Service
A document filed by a process server or authorized person confirming that legal papers (citation and petition) were served on the respondent. The Return of Service must state the date, time, place, and manner of service and must be filed with the court. An improperly completed Return of Service can delay proceedings and may result in a default judgment being set aside. The platform tracks Return of Service as a required workflow milestone and validates its filing before allowing the case to proceed past the service stage.

## S

### SAPCR (Suit Affecting the Parent-Child Relationship)
A lawsuit filed under Texas Family Code Title 5 to establish, modify, or enforce orders regarding conservatorship, possession and access, child support, and medical support for a child. SAPCR cases may be filed independently (not as part of a divorce) by parents, the Attorney General, or other parties with standing under Texas Family Code § 102.003-102.006. SAPCR is the procedural vehicle for virtually all child-related litigation in Texas, including paternity establishment, custody disputes, child support enforcement, and grandparent access claims.

### Service of Process
The formal delivery of legal documents (citation and petition) to a party to provide notice of a legal proceeding. In Texas, service must comply with Rules 99-107 of the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure. Methods of service include: personal service by a constable, sheriff, or private process server; service by certified mail (with restrictions); service by publication (when the respondent cannot be located); and substituted service (authorized by court order). Proper service is a jurisdictional prerequisite — without it, the court lacks personal jurisdiction over the respondent.

### Social Study
An investigation and written report ordered by the court to evaluate the home environment, parenting capacity, and best interests of a child in a conservatorship dispute. In Harris County, social studies are commonly conducted by the Domestic Relations Office (DRO) or by private licensed professionals. The social study typically includes home visits, parent interviews, child interviews, criminal background checks, and reference checks. The resulting report is filed with the court and is often influential in the court's conservatorship determination. See also: DRO.

### Standing Order
A general order issued by a court that applies automatically to all cases of a certain type filed in that court. In Harris County, the family district courts have a Standing Order that goes into effect upon the filing of a divorce or SAPCR suit. The Harris County Standing Order typically includes mutual injunctions prohibiting both parties from dissipating assets, hiding property, disturbing the peace, removing children from the jurisdiction, and making disparaging remarks about the other party to or in the presence of children. The Standing Order is effective immediately upon filing (for the petitioner) and upon service (for the respondent), without the need for a separate hearing.

## T

### Temporary Orders
Court orders that govern the rights and obligations of the parties during the pendency of a case (before final orders are entered). In Texas family law, temporary orders commonly address: temporary conservatorship and possession of children, temporary child support, exclusive use of the marital residence, payment of bills and debts, injunctive provisions, and attorney's fees. Temporary orders are entered after a hearing (or by agreement) and remain in effect until modified by subsequent temporary orders or superseded by final orders.

### TRO (Temporary Restraining Order)
An emergency court order issued without notice to the opposing party (ex parte) that provides immediate, short-term protection. In Texas family law, TROs are commonly issued in protective order cases (family violence) and in divorce cases (to prevent asset dissipation). A TRO is effective for a limited period (typically 14 days, extendable to 28 days) and must be followed by a hearing for temporary orders or a protective order. The platform must flag TRO deadlines for the hearing and ensure timely scheduling.

### Trust Account
A bank account maintained by a law firm to hold client funds separate from the firm's operating funds. In Texas, trust accounts must be maintained in compliance with the Texas Disciplinary Rules of Professional Conduct (Rule 1.14). Client funds in trust include retainers, settlement proceeds, and funds received on behalf of clients. The platform maintains a detailed trust ledger for each client, tracks deposits and disbursements, and generates trust account reconciliation reports. See also: IOLTA, Retainer.

## W

### Waiver of Service
A document signed by the respondent voluntarily waiving formal service of process (citation). In Texas, a Waiver of Service must comply with Texas Family Code § 6.4035 (for divorces) and Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 119. The waiver must be signed after the petition has been filed, must be sworn before a notary, and must include specific statutory language. A Waiver of Service simplifies and accelerates the case timeline by eliminating the need for formal service through a constable or process server.

### Writ of Withholding
A judicial writ issued under Texas Family Code Chapter 158 directing an employer to withhold earnings from an obligor's income for child support or spousal maintenance. The Writ of Withholding is the enforcement mechanism that puts an Income Withholding Order into effect. It is served on the employer and requires withholding to begin no later than the first pay period after receipt. The writ includes the amount to be withheld, the payment address (typically the State Disbursement Unit), and the obligor's identifying information. See also: Income Withholding Order.

## Additional Terms

### Engagement Letter
See entry above under **E**.

### Filing Code
A standardized code used in the eFileTexas system to classify the type of document being filed. Each document type (Original Petition, Motion, Order, etc.) has a corresponding filing code that determines routing, filing fees, and clerk processing. ### Financial Information Statement (FIS)
A sworn statement of a party's income, expenses, assets, and liabilities, required by many Harris County family courts in divorce and child support cases. The FIS is typically filed as part of temporary orders or discovery and provides the court with the financial information necessary to calculate child support, divide property, and award spousal maintenance.

### Net Resources
The calculation base for Texas child support guidelines, defined in Texas Family Code § 154.062. Net resources include all wage and salary income, self-employment income, interest, dividends, rental income, and other sources of income, minus social security taxes, federal income tax, state income tax, union dues, and health insurance premiums for the child. The platform must calculate net resources as part of its child support worksheet functionality.

### State Disbursement Unit (SDU)
The centralized unit operated by the Texas Attorney General's Office that processes child support payments. All income withholding payments and most direct child support payments are routed through the SDU. The SDU tracks payments, maintains payment records, and disburses funds to the custodial parent or other payee.
